# Genre Conventions: Obligatory Scenes & Reader Expectations

## Understanding Genre

Genre is a **contract with the reader**. Each genre has:
- **Obligatory Scenes** - Events readers EXPECT
- **Conventions** - Elements readers ASSUME will be present
- **Values at Stake** - Core human needs being tested

**Your job:** Fulfill the contract with fresh execution, or subvert it brilliantly (while still satisfying the underlying need).

---

## The Ten Save the Cat Story Genres

### 1. WHYDUNIT (Mystery/Investigation)

**Core Value:** Truth vs. Deception

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Discovery of the crime/mystery
- False trail/red herring pursuit
- Dark turn where investigator is implicated or threatened
- Revelation of the truth (often public)

**Key Character:**
The **Detective** who must expose the truth

**Conventions:**
- Clues hidden in plain sight
- Multiple suspects
- The least likely suspect often guilty
- Justice (or deliberate injustice) at end

**Transformation:**
Detective often learns something about human nature or themselves

---

### 2. RITES OF PASSAGE (Coming of Age)

**Core Value:** Innocence vs. Experience

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Life change forces protagonist out of comfort zone
- Wrong path chosen (avoidance of change)
- Rock bottom / acceptance of reality
- Emergence as changed person

**Key Character:**
The **Protagonist in Transition** between life phases

**Conventions:**
- Mentor figure (often imperfect)
- Separation from family/familiar
- Test of identity
- Loss of something valued (innocence, person, belief)

**Transformation:**
Protagonist moves from one life phase to another (childhood→adulthood, single→married, etc.)

---

### 3. INSTITUTIONALIZED (Group Dynamics)

**Core Value:** Individual vs. Institution

**Subtypes:**
- **Military Unit** - Group fighting external enemy
- **Dysfunctional Family** - Blood ties under pressure
- **Business/Organization** - Workplace as battleground

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Protagonist joins the group (willing or forced)
- Testing/initiation
- Sacrifice for group (or betrayal of group)
- Decision: join permanently, reform, or leave

**Key Character:**
The **Insider/Outsider** navigating group belonging

**Conventions:**
- Group has its own rules/language/culture
- Individual must compromise to belong
- The group offers something the individual needs
- Cost of belonging vs. cost of isolation

**Transformation:**
Protagonist finds place (or definitively rejects one)

---

### 4. SUPERHERO (Extraordinary Person)

**Core Value:** Responsibility vs. Self-Interest

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Discovery/acceptance of special powers
- Initial triumph
- "I give up" moment (power is burden)
- Sacrifice for greater good
- Final confrontation requiring full power + growth

**Key Character:**
The **Gifted One** who must choose how to use their gifts

**Conventions:**
- Nemesis who is dark mirror of hero
- Power comes with cost
- Secret identity / dual life tension
- Origin story (how powers emerged)

**Transformation:**
Hero learns responsibility accompanies power

---

### 5. DUDE WITH A PROBLEM (Ordinary Person/Extraordinary Situation)

**Core Value:** Survival vs. Surrender

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Ordinary world established
- Problem arrives (uninvited, overwhelming)
- Initial efforts fail
- Helper/tool discovered
- Final confrontation using learned skills

**Key Character:**
The **Everyman** thrust into impossible circumstances

**Conventions:**
- Protagonist is ORDINARY (we could be them)
- Problem is EXTRAORDINARY (we fear it)
- No one is coming to help (protagonist must act)
- MacGyver moments (using available resources)

**Transformation:**
Ordinary person discovers extraordinary capacity within

---

### 6. FOOL TRIUMPHANT (Underdog Victory)

**Core Value:** Worth vs. Worthlessness

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Fool is dismissed/underestimated
- Fool's pure heart enables unique insight
- Establishment tries to stop fool
- Fool wins through authenticity, not conventional means

**Key Character:**
The **Underdog** who has nothing but themselves

**Conventions:**
- Powerful establishment antagonist
- Fool has quality others lack (innocence, kindness)
- Comic moments of collision with "serious" world
- Victory redefines what "winning" means

**Transformation:**
Fool's value is proven; world shifts to accommodate them

---

### 7. BUDDY LOVE (Relationship as Catalyst)

**Core Value:** Love vs. Hate / Connection vs. Isolation

**Subtypes:**
- **Romantic Love** - Two lovers
- **Buddy Cop** - Two mismatched partners
- **Bromance/Friendship** - Platonic deep bond
- **Pet Love** - Human and animal

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- The "meet cute" or forced pairing
- Complication that forces proximity
- Betrayal or major misunderstanding
- "All is lost" separation
- Sacrifice that proves love
- Reunion

**Key Character:**
The **Incomplete One** made whole through connection

**Conventions:**
- Opposites attract
- Initial conflict/dislike
- External threat tests relationship
- Characters change each other

**Transformation:**
Each character becomes fuller through relationship

---

### 8. OUT OF THE BOTTLE (Wish/Curse)

**Core Value:** Gratitude vs. Dissatisfaction

**Subtypes:**
- **Body Switch** - In someone else's life
- **Angel Story** - Supernatural helper
- **Magic Object** - Device with power
- **Curse** - Unwanted transformation

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Wish/curse granted (be careful what you wish for)
- Apparent victory/enjoyment
- Dark side of wish emerges
- Desperate attempt to reverse
- Return to normal WITH new appreciation

**Key Character:**
The **Dissatisfied One** who must learn gratitude

**Conventions:**
- Magic has rules/limitations
- Wish reveals character flaws
- "The grass isn't greener"
- Lesson about appreciating what you have

**Transformation:**
Character learns to value their real life

---

### 9. GOLDEN FLEECE (Quest/Journey)

**Core Value:** Growth vs. Stagnation

**Subtypes:**
- **Epic Quest** - Journey for object/destination
- **Sports Story** - Journey to championship
- **Caper/Heist** - Journey to complete score
- **Road Trip** - Journey as transformation vehicle

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Team assembled
- Journey begins
- Obstacles on the road
- False victory/defeat
- Dark moment far from home
- Final push to goal
- Return transformed

**Key Character:**
The **Seeker** searching for something

**Conventions:**
- The journey matters more than destination
- Ensemble cast (road buddies)
- Lessons learned at each stop
- What's sought is NOT what's needed

**Transformation:**
Seeker finds what they actually need (not what they wanted)

---

### 10. MONSTER IN THE HOUSE (Horror/Thriller)

**Core Value:** Life vs. Death

**Requirements:**
- **Monster** - Supernatural, human, or metaphorical threat
- **House** - Enclosed space (physical or situational)
- **Sin** - Reason the monster is unleashed (someone did something wrong)

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Sin is committed (often before or early in story)
- Monster awakens/appears
- Initial attack (monster is REAL and dangerous)
- False victory (think it's beaten)
- Final confrontation (often in most confined space)

**Key Character:**
The **Survivor(s)** who must face primal fear

**Conventions:**
- Monster reflects human evil/fear
- Isolation/confinement increases terror
- Rules for defeating monster (learned gradually)
- Sacrifice required (who dies so others live?)

**Transformation:**
Survivors face their deepest fears; some don't survive

## Additional Genre Considerations

### LITERARY FICTION

**Core Value:** Meaning vs. Meaninglessness

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Epiphany moment (even if subtle)
- Symbolic resonance
- Ambiguity that provokes thought

**Conventions:**
- Character-driven plot
- Thematic complexity
- Prose as art
- Open or ambiguous ending acceptable

### THRILLER

**Core Value:** Safety vs. Danger

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Hero at mercy of villain
- False victory that reverses
- Ticking clock creates pressure
- Final confrontation

**Conventions:**
- High stakes (life/death or equivalent)
- Worthy opponent
- Twists and reversals
- Action sequences

### ROMANCE

**Core Value:** Love vs. Loneliness

**Obligatory Scenes:**
- Meet-cute (characters first interact)
- "I hate you but I can't stop thinking about you"
- First kiss/intimacy
- Grand gesture
- Union (HEA/HFN)

**Conventions:**
- Two protagonists (dual POV often)
- Clear obstacles to love
- Sexual tension escalates
- Emotionally satisfying ending required

## Blending Genres

Most successful stories blend genres:
- **External Genre** - Surface plot (thriller, mystery, etc.)
- **Internal Genre** - Character arc (coming of age, redemption, etc.)

Example: *The Hunger Games*
- External: Monster in the House (arena as house, other tributes + Gamemakers as monster)
- Internal: Rites of Passage (Katniss's transformation from survivor to symbol)

**When blending:**
1. Choose primary genre (delivers main promises)
2. Add secondary genre (deepens character/theme)
3. Ensure both genres' obligatory scenes are present
4. Let internal genre drive the WHY of external genre events
