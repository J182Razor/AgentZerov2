# Scene Craft: Building Powerful Scenes

## What Makes a Scene

A **scene** is a unit of story taking place in:
- One location (or continuous movement)
- One time span (no significant breaks)
- Containing one **value shift**

**If no value shifts, it's not a scene—it's exposition wearing a scene's clothing.**

---

## The Value Shift

Every scene must **turn on a value**—something must change from beginning to end.

### Types of Value Shifts:

**Positive to Negative:**
- Hope → Despair
- Love → Hate
- Safety → Danger
- Trust → Betrayal

**Negative to Positive:**
- Despair → Hope
- Ignorance → Knowledge
- Conflict → Resolution
- Danger → Safety

**Positive to More Positive:**
- Good → Great

**Negative to More Negative:**
- Bad → Worse

**Negation of the Negation (Ironic):**
- Tyranny → Open Revolt → Tyranny Disguised as Freedom

### Tracking Value Shifts:

For each scene, note:
- **Value at Start:** [specific emotional/situational state]
- **Value at End:** [changed state]
- **What Changed It:** [the turning point]

---

## Scene Structure

### Beginning: The Hook

The scene opening must:
- Establish where/when we are
- Show characters in action
- Raise a question or goal
- Create immediate interest

**Start in medias res**—action already happening.

### Middle: Progressive Complication

The scene middle must:
- Escalate tension
- Introduce obstacles
- Create gaps between expectation and result
- Push toward the turning point

**Each beat raises stakes or shifts power.**

### End: The Turning Point

The scene climax must:
- Deliver the value shift
- Often contain a decision/action
- Change the situation
- Point to what comes next

**Scenes should END when the value turns—don't linger.**

## The Gap

The most powerful tool in scene craft is **the gap**:

**Expectation → Gap → Result**

When a character acts, they expect a result. The **gap** is the space between what they expected and what actually happened.

### Example:
- **Expectation:** She says "I love you," expecting him to say it back
- **Gap:** He doesn't respond
- **Result:** She realizes he doesn't feel the same

### Using Gaps:

- Put characters in situations where they must act
- Have them choose actions based on their worldview
- Let reality contradict their expectations
- Force them to take new action in response

**Gaps create authentic character behavior—they must respond to the unexpected.**

## Scene Goals and Obstacles

### Every POV Character Needs:

**GOAL:** What they want from this scene
**OBSTACLE:** What prevents them from getting it
**STAKES:** What happens if they fail

### Scene Goal Types:

1. **Concrete Goal** - Get object, reach location
2. **Information Goal** - Learn something specific
3. **Emotional Goal** - Receive validation, connection
4. **Relational Goal** - Change how someone sees them
5. **Avoidance Goal** - Prevent something from happening

### Obstacles Must Be:

- **Appropriate** - Matched to the goal
- **Escalating** - Harder as scene progresses
- **Meaningful** - Tied to larger story stakes
- **Active** - Not just circumstance (usually another character)

---

## Scene Types

### 1. PROACTIVE Scene

- Character pursues a goal
- Takes initiative
- Drives the plot forward
- Hero scenes are often proactive

### 2. REACTIVE Scene

- Character responds to event
- Processes what happened
- Decides what to do next
- Often follow major plot turns

### 3. REVELATION Scene

- Information delivered
- Changes understanding
- Sets up new action
- Must still have tension/conflict

### 4. CONFRONTATION Scene

- Direct conflict between characters
- Power shifts
- Major value changes
- Often climactic

### 5. TRANSITION Scene

- Moves between locations/times
- Usually compressed
- Still needs micro-tension
- Avoid lingering

## Scene Cards

### The Scene Card Format:

```
SCENE #: [Title]
POV: [Whose head are we in?]
LOCATION: [Where?]
TIME: [When?]

WHAT HAPPENS: [External plot summary]

WHY IT MATTERS: [Third rail connection]

VALUE SHIFT: [Starting state] → [Ending state]

GOAL: [What POV character wants]
OBSTACLE: [What prevents it]
OUTCOME: [Success/Fail/Partial + complication]

CAUSE: [What scene triggered this one?]
EFFECT: [What does this scene trigger?]
```

### Using Scene Cards:

1. Create card for every scene
2. Verify value shift exists
3. Check third rail connection
4. Ensure cause→effect linkage
5. Rearrange to test sequence

## The But-and-Therefore Test

Replace "AND THEN" connections with "BUT" or "THEREFORE":

**Weak (And Then):**
- She went to the store AND THEN went home AND THEN ate dinner

**Strong (But/Therefore):**
- She went to the store BUT it was closed, THEREFORE she had to find another option, BUT...

### In Scene Sequences:

Scene A happens → **THEREFORE** → Scene B must happen
Scene B happens → **BUT** → unexpected complication
**THEREFORE** → Scene C attempts to address complication

**Every "but" = conflict. Every "therefore" = causality.**

## The Hourglass

When you have your audience's attention, **slow down**.

### When to Use Hourglasses:

- Before major revelations
- During emotional peaks
- At moments of high tension
- Just before climactic action

### How to Slow Down:

1. **Add sensory detail** - What does it look, smell, sound, feel like?
2. **Extend physical action** - Slow-motion the important moment
3. **Use shorter sentences** - Creates reading pace shift
4. **Add internal reaction** - Character's thoughts/feelings
5. **Use description** - The environment, objects, light

### Example:

**Before Hourglass:**
> She knocked on the door and he answered.

**After Hourglass:**
> The blue paint was chipped at the edges, revealing layers beneath—white, then green, then the raw wood grain. She raised her hand. Her knuckles hovered an inch from the surface. Inside, someone was moving. A chair scraped against a floor. Footsteps approached.
> 
> She knocked.
> 
> The footsteps stopped. Then continued, faster now. The lock turned—a heavy click that seemed to fill the empty street. The door swung inward, and there he was.

## Stakes in Scenes

Every scene needs **micro-stakes** (what's at risk in this scene) connected to **macro-stakes** (what's at risk in the story).

### Raising Scene Stakes:

1. **Make it personal** - Connect to character's deepest need
2. **Add a clock** - Limited time to achieve goal
3. **Increase consequences** - Failure means X worse thing
4. **Add witnesses** - Public failure/success
5. **Remove options** - This is the only way

### The Five Stakes Elephants:

When any scene feels low-stakes, add one of:
1. **Physical danger** - Threat to body
2. **Professional danger** - Threat to career/livelihood
3. **Social danger** - Threat to reputation/relationships
4. **Psychological danger** - Threat to identity/sanity
5. **Existential danger** - Threat to meaning/purpose

## Scene Transitions

### Between Scenes:

- **Cut** - Abrupt end, new scene starts (most common)
- **Dissolve** - Soft transition, time passes
- **Match** - Visual/thematic link between scenes

### Compression:

Skip over:
- Travel time
- Sleep
- Routine activity
- Anything without value shift

**Cut to the next interesting thing.**

### Expansion:

Slow down for:
- Emotional moments
- Revelations
- Confrontations
- Decision points

---

## Dialogue in Scenes

### Dialogue Should:

- Reveal character
- Advance plot
- Create conflict
- Contain subtext
- Feel natural

### Dialogue Should NOT:

- Explain things characters already know
- State themes directly
- Sound like a speech
- Be perfectly eloquent
- Say exactly what's meant

### Subtext Principle:

**Characters rarely say what they mean.**

| Surface | Subtext |
|---------|---------|
| "Fine." | "I'm furious." |
| "Whatever you think is best." | "I've given up." |
| "That's an interesting choice." | "You're an idiot." |

## Sensory Grounding

Each scene needs **sensory anchoring**:

### The Five Senses:

1. **Sight** - Colors, light, shapes, movement
2. **Sound** - Dialogue, ambient noise, music, silence
3. **Smell** - Often forgotten, very evocative
4. **Touch** - Temperature, texture, pressure
5. **Taste** - Food, drink, blood, air

### Choosing Details:

Select sensory details that:
- **Reveal character** - What would THIS person notice?
- **Establish mood** - What atmosphere do you need?
- **Foreshadow** - What will matter later?
- **Ground the reader** - Where/when are we?

**Specific beats general: "A 1982 Datsun B210" not "an old car"**

## Checklist Before Writing a Scene

- [ ] Who is the POV character?
- [ ] What is their scene goal?
- [ ] What obstacle opposes them?
- [ ] What are the stakes of this scene?
- [ ] What value shifts?
- [ ] How does this touch the third rail?
- [ ] What causes this scene?
- [ ] What does this scene cause?
- [ ] Where's the turning point?
- [ ] Do I start with action?
- [ ] Do I end when the value turns?
