# Prose Style: Line-Level Craft

## The Sentence as Unit of Meaning

Every sentence should:
- Advance the story (plot, character, or theme)
- Create rhythm with surrounding sentences
- Contain fresh, specific language
- Avoid redundancy

**If a sentence does none of these, cut it.**

---

## Show, Don't Tell (But Know When to Tell)

### SHOWING = Scene

- Action happening in real time
- Dialogue spoken
- Sensory details experienced
- Reader draws conclusions

**Example:** "He slammed his fist on the table. The glasses jumped."

### TELLING = Summary

- Narrator explains
- Time compressed
- Reader given conclusions
- Necessary for transitions

**Example:** "He was an angry man, quick to rage."

### When to Show:

- Important moments
- Character-revealing scenes
- Emotional peaks
- Transformation points

### When to Tell:

- Transitions
- Backstory snippets
- Time compression
- Setup information

**Balance:** Major moments = SHOW. Connective tissue = TELL.

## The Cinema of the Mind

Create vivid mental movies through:

### Specific Details

**Vague:** "He drove an old car."
**Specific:** "He drove a 1976 Datsun B210 with a cracked windshield and a driver's seat held together with duct tape."

### Active Verbs

**Weak:** "She was moving quickly across the room."
**Strong:** "She darted across the room."

**Weak:** "The sound of thunder was in the distance."
**Strong:** "Thunder rumbled in the distance."

### Concrete Nouns

**Abstract:** "He felt a strong emotion."
**Concrete:** "His throat tightened. His hands shook."

### Physical Actions for Emotions

**Telling:** "She was nervous."
**Showing:** "She twisted her ring. Her eyes flicked toward the door."

## Rhythm and Pacing

### Sentence Length Controls Pace:

**Long sentences** = Slower pace, contemplation, description
**Short sentences** = Faster pace, action, impact

### Example:

**Slow (contemplative):**
> The morning light came through the window in dusty columns, illuminating the photographs on her desk, the coffee cup with its ring of yesterday's grounds, the stack of papers she had been avoiding for three days, each one representing a conversation she didn't want to have.

**Fast (action):**
> She heard the footsteps. The door flew open. He was there. Gun raised.

### Sentence Variety:

Vary structure to avoid monotony:
- Simple sentences (Subject-Verb-Object)
- Compound sentences (clause + clause)
- Complex sentences (subordinate + main)
- Fragments (For. Effect.)
- Questions (What now?)

## Voice and POV

### First Person

**"I went to the store."**

**Strengths:**
- Immediate intimacy
- Strong character voice
- Limited knowledge creates mystery

**Challenges:**
- Narrator must be in every scene
- Risk of too much interiority
- How does narrator know what they know?

### Third Person Limited

**"She went to the store."**

**Strengths:**
- Close to character
- Can shift between characters
- More flexible than first person

**Challenges:**
- Must stay in one head per scene
- Easy to accidentally slip into omniscience
- Voice must still be distinctive

### Third Person Omniscient

**"She went to the store, unaware that he was following."**

**Strengths:**
- God's-eye view
- Can show all perspectives
- Full access to information

**Challenges:**
- Harder to create intimacy
- Risk of "head-hopping"
- Less common in contemporary fiction

### Maintaining POV Discipline:

- Only show what POV character can see/hear/know
- Filter all descriptions through their perception
- Their vocabulary, not yours
- Their interpretation, not objective reality

## Dialogue Craft

### Dialogue Tags

**Default to "said"** - It's invisible to readers

**Use action beats** instead of tags:
> "I don't think so." She turned away from the window.

**Avoid:**
- Said synonyms (exclaimed, uttered, queried)
- Adverbs modifying tags ("said angrily")
- Tags every line (let rhythm identify speaker)

### Dialogue Rhythm

**Vary line lengths:**
> "No."
> "Why not?"
> "Because I've spent the last twenty years doing exactly what everyone expected, and I'm tired of it. I'm tired of being the version of myself that makes everyone else comfortable."
> "Oh."

### Subtext

**What's said ≠ What's meant**

Surface conversation / Real conversation:
- "How are you?" / "I need something from you."
- "Fine." / "I don't trust you."
- "That's interesting." / "You're wrong but I won't say so."

### Dialect and Idiolect

**Dialect** = Regional/cultural speech patterns
**Idiolect** = Individual speech habits

Use sparingly:
- Suggests voice without phonetic spelling
- Key phrases or word choices
- Rhythm more than spelling

**Don't:** "Ah ain't goin' thar wit' yew."
**Do:** "I'm not going there with you." He dropped the 'g' on 'going' like everyone from that part of Kentucky.

## Description and Detail

### Selection Principle

**Not everything—the RIGHT things**

Choose details that:
1. Reveal character (what would THEY notice?)
2. Establish mood
3. Advance theme
4. Ground the reader in place/time

### The Rule of Three

Three specific details create a vivid impression:
> The kitchen: peeling linoleum, a mountain of dishes in the sink, the smell of bacon grease gone cold.

### Motif Repetition

Repeat meaningful images throughout:
- Colors (Gatsby's green light)
- Objects (a wedding ring)
- Sounds (a specific song)
- Actions (a particular gesture)

## Prose Temperature

### LEAN Prose

- Short sentences
- Minimal adjectives
- Action-focused
- Hemingway, Carver

**Example:**
> He sat. He drank. He waited. The door opened.

### LUSH Prose

- Long sentences
- Rich description
- Language as art
- McCarthy, Morrison

**Example:**
> The last light of the dying day fell across the water in sheets of amber and copper, turning the mundane into something almost too beautiful to comprehend, a reminder that even ordinary moments carry within them the possibility of transcendence.

**Choose based on:**
- Genre expectations
- Character voice
- Scene needs
- Your natural style

## Common Prose Problems

### Filtering

**Problem:** Unnecessarily filtering through POV character

**Filtering:** "She saw the car pull into the driveway."
**Direct:** "The car pulled into the driveway."

**Filtering:** "He heard the phone ring."
**Direct:** "The phone rang."

### Purple Prose

**Problem:** Overwriting

**Purple:** "The scintillating orb of radiance descended beyond the horizon, painting the firmament in hues of amber and vermillion."
**Cleaner:** "The sun set. The sky went orange, then red."

### Redundancy

**Problem:** Saying the same thing twice

**Redundant:** "She nodded her head."
**Better:** "She nodded."

**Redundant:** "He shrugged his shoulders."
**Better:** "He shrugged."

### Passive Voice

**Passive:** "The ball was thrown by him."
**Active:** "He threw the ball."

**Use passive when:**
- The actor is unknown
- The receiver matters more than actor
- Intentional distancing

### Weak Verbs + Adverbs

**Weak:** "She walked quickly."
**Strong:** "She hurried." or "She rushed."

**Weak:** "He said angrily."
**Strong:** "He snapped." or "'I don't think so.' He slammed his hand on the table."

## First Lines and Last Lines

### First Lines Must:

- Hook attention
- Establish voice
- Raise questions
- Set tone
- Be remarkable enough to remember

**Examples:**
- "It was a bright cold day in April, and the clocks were striking thirteen." (*1984*)
- "Call me Ishmael." (*Moby-Dick*)
- "Happy families are all alike; every unhappy family is unhappy in its own way." (*Anna Karenina*)

### Last Lines Must:

- Resonate with theme
- Provide closure (or intentional ambiguity)
- Echo beginning (transformed)
- Be memorable

**Last lines often:**
- Circle back to opening image
- State theme (now earned)
- Project into future
- Leave emotional resonance

## Revision Checklist

### First Pass: Story

- [ ] Does every scene need to be here?
- [ ] Does every scene have a value shift?
- [ ] Is causality clear (but/therefore)?
- [ ] Are stakes escalating?

### Second Pass: Character

- [ ] Is voice consistent?
- [ ] Are characters distinguishable in dialogue?
- [ ] Is interiority meaningful (not excessive)?
- [ ] Do characters act according to their nature?

### Third Pass: Prose

- [ ] Are sentences varied in length/structure?
- [ ] Is there filtering to remove?
- [ ] Are weak verbs strengthened?
- [ ] Are details specific (not generic)?

### Fourth Pass: Polish

- [ ] Read aloud for rhythm
- [ ] Cut redundancies
- [ ] Strengthen first/last lines of scenes
- [ ] Check continuity details
