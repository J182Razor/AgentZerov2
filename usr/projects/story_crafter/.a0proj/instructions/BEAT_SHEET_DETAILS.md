# Save the Cat Beat Sheet: Detailed Guide with Examples

## Understanding the Transformation Machine

The beat sheet is a **transformation machine**—a system designed to reprogram heroes. Think of protagonists as robots operating by flawed programming. The beat sheet shows which wires to cut, which code to alter, and in what order.

## The Five Foundation Beats

Start your outline with these five **directional beats** that form the story's pillars:

1. **Catalyst** - What kicks hero out of status quo?
2. **Break Into 2** - What is the new world like?
3. **Midpoint** - False victory or false defeat?
4. **All Is Lost** - What rock-bottom event forces change?
5. **Break Into 3** - What aha moment enables transformation?

---

## Beat-by-Beat Deep Dive

### 1. OPENING IMAGE (0-1%)

**Purpose:** Visual "before" snapshot establishing hero and world BEFORE transformation.

**Must Include:**
- One or more character flaws visible through action
- Tone, mood, and genre indicators
- Single scene or interconnected scene group
- SHOWING, not telling

**Examples:**
- *The Hunger Games*: Katniss wakes in her sparse bed, her district's poverty evident, her mother's depression visible
- *The Martian*: Mark Watney abandoned on Mars, presumed dead, utterly alone
- *Me Before You*: Louisa comfortable in her small English town, trapped but unaware

**Questions to Ask:**
- Is this one scene or connected scenes?
- Are flaws evident through behavior, not narration?
- Would a reader understand the transformation if they compared this to the Final Image?

---

### 2. THEME STATED (5%)

**Purpose:** Someone plants the seed of what the hero must learn.

**Must Include:**
- Delivered by someone OTHER than the hero
- Hero easily dismisses it
- Directly relates to the hero's NEED (not want)

**Examples:**
- *E.T.*: Elliot's brother yells "Why don't you grow up and learn how other people feel?"
- *The Iron Giant*: "You are who you choose to be"
- *Jaws*: Hints at facing fear vs. running from it

**The Dismissal is Key:** If the hero accepted the theme immediately, there would be no story. The theme must bounce off the hero's misbelief.

---

### 3. SETUP (1-10%)

**Purpose:** Fully establish the "thesis world" that will be upended.

**Must Include:**
- At least one "thing that needs fixing"
- A Story characters (primary cast)
- Hero's WANT/external goal
- Hero shown in multiple life areas
- Visible flaws in action
- Stasis = death pressure (life cannot continue this way)

**Things That Need Fixing:**
Show problems in the hero's life that transformation will address:
- Broken relationships
- Fear controlling decisions
- Self-destructive patterns
- Unfulfilled potential

**Stasis = Death:**
Even if the hero doesn't know it, continuing unchanged leads to destruction. Plant this urgency.

---

### 4. CATALYST (10%)

**Purpose:** The event that makes the status quo impossible.

**Critical Rules:**
- Must be an **ACTION** beat (something HAPPENS)
- Cannot be a decision or revelation
- Must happen TO the hero
- Big enough to prevent return to normal
- Often arrives as "bad news" disguised as opportunity (or vice versa)

**Examples:**
- *Legally Blonde*: Elle gets dumped (happens TO her)
- *The Hunger Games*: Prim's name drawn (happens TO Katniss)
- *Jaws*: Shark kills swimmer (happens TO Brody's town)

**Not a Catalyst:**
- Hero decides to do something different
- Hero learns information
- Hero has a realization

These can happen AFTER the Catalyst, but the Catalyst itself must be an external event.

---

### 5. DEBATE (10-20%)

**Purpose:** Hero wrestles with whether to accept the call.

**Forms it Takes:**
- Question form: "Should I go? Should I stay? Should I accept?"
- Preparation form: Hero prepares for inevitable change
- Both: Questioning while preparing

**Must Show:**
- Hesitation and reluctance
- Weighing pros and cons
- Fear of the unknown
- Attachment to status quo

**Why It Matters:**
Without Debate, the hero seems eager for change. But people RESIST change, even beneficial change. Debate makes the hero human.

---

### 6. BREAK INTO 2 (20%)

**Purpose:** The decisive moment when the hero commits to the new world.

**Critical Elements:**
- Must be PROACTIVE choice (hero decides)
- Act 2 world is OPPOSITE of Act 1 world
- Decision based on WANT (the wrong way to change)
- Clear threshold crossing

**Upside-Down World:**
Whatever the Act 1 world was, Act 2 is its inverse:
- Familiar → Strange
- Safe → Dangerous
- Alone → With others
- Stuck → In motion
- Comfortable → Challenging

**The Wrong Way:**
The hero enters Act 2 trying to solve their problem the WRONG way—pursuing what they WANT rather than what they NEED. This ensures further struggle.

---

### 7. B STORY (22%)

**Purpose:** Introduce the character(s) who will help the hero learn the theme.

**B Story Characters:**
- Love interest
- Mentor
- Nemesis who reflects truth
- New friend/ally
- Helper figure

**Theme Connection:**
The B Story character EMBODIES the theme in some way. They:
- Have already learned what the hero must learn
- Represent what the hero could become
- Challenge the hero's misbelief
- Provide the insight needed for Break Into 3

**Product of Act 2:**
The B Story character belongs in the "upside-down" world—they would seem strange in Act 1.

---

### 8. FUN AND GAMES (20-50%)

**Purpose:** Deliver the promise of the premise—why readers picked up this book.

**This Section Answers:**
- What does this genre promise?
- What did the concept/hook promise?
- What do readers want to experience?

**Two Paths:**
1. **Succeeding/Loving It** - Hero excels in new world (leads to false victory midpoint)
2. **Floundering/Hating It** - Hero struggles in new world (leads to false defeat midpoint)

**The Trailer Moments:**
If your book were a movie, the trailer would feature Fun and Games scenes. These are the genre-delivering, hook-fulfilling moments.

---

### 9. MIDPOINT (50%)

**Purpose:** Raise stakes and shift hero's direction.

**Two Types:**

**False Victory:**
- Hero appears to succeed
- Gets what they WANT
- Stakes raise (now there's more to lose)
- Party or celebration moment
- "But wait, there's more..."

**False Defeat:**
- Hero appears to fail
- Loses what they wanted
- Stakes raise (must try harder)
- Low point before push
- "Can't give up now..."

**A and B Stories Cross:**
At Midpoint, the external (A) and internal (B) stories intersect. The B Story character often catalyzes the shift.

**Shift from Want to Need:**
Even subtly, the Midpoint begins turning the hero from pursuing WANT toward pursuing NEED.

---

### 10. BAD GUYS CLOSE IN (50-75%)

**Purpose:** The consequences of the false victory/defeat cascade.

**If Midpoint Was False Victory:**
Things get WORSE:
- Success brings new problems
- External enemies attack
- Internal flaws create failures
- What the hero wanted proves hollow

**If Midpoint Was False Defeat:**
Things seem to get BETTER:
- Hero rallies and succeeds
- But it's fragile, temporary
- Seeds of All Is Lost are planted
- Success built on wrong foundation

**Internal Bad Guys:**
The hero's FLAWS are the real antagonists. External enemies only matter because they expose internal weakness.

---

### 11. ALL IS LOST (75%)

**Purpose:** The lowest point—rock bottom that forces transformation.

**Must Include:**
- An ACTION beat (something HAPPENS to hero)
- Whiff of death (literal or metaphorical)
- Feels like another Catalyst
- Combined with internal flaws = devastation

**Whiff of Death:**
Something associated with death appears:
- Actual death of mentor/ally
- Near-death experience
- Metaphorical death (divorce, job loss, reputation destroyed)
- Symbolic death imagery

**All Is Lost + Internal Bad Guys:**
The external All Is Lost event combines with the hero's internal flaws to create total devastation.

---

### 12. DARK NIGHT OF THE SOUL (75-80%)

**Purpose:** Hero processes everything and reaches their lowest emotional point.

**Characteristics:**
- Reaction beat (not action)
- Reflection and wallowing
- Hero worse off than story's start
- Darkest before dawn
- Seeds of epiphany planted

**This Is Not:**
- New plot events
- More bad things happening
- Action sequences

**This Is:**
- Processing
- Grieving
- Questioning everything
- Ready to give up

---

### 13. BREAK INTO 3 (80%)

**Purpose:** The "aha!" moment—hero sees what they must really do.

**Must Include:**
- Universal life lesson learned (theme realized)
- Proactive decision to fix things
- Decision based on NEED (the right way to change)
- Synthesis of Act 1 and Act 2 worlds

**The Synthesis:**
Act 3 combines the best of both worlds:
- Act 1: Who the hero was
- Act 2: Who they became
- Act 3: Who they must be (integration)

**Theme Realized:**
The dismissed theme from 5% is now understood. The hero "gets it."

---

### 14. FINALE (80-99%)

**Purpose:** Hero proves transformation is real through action.

**The Five-Point Finale:**

**Point 1: Gathering the Team**
Hero assembles:
- Allies
- Resources
- Information
- Courage
May include "storming the castle" approach

**Point 2: Executing the Plan**
Initial assault:
- Hero and team attack the problem
- Early success
- Confidence building

**Point 3: High Tower Surprise**
The twist:
- Plan was never going to work
- New complication emerges
- Hero led into trap
- Everything falls apart

**Point 4: Dig Deep Down**
The real test:
- Hero must use THEME to survive
- Inner transformation becomes weapon
- No external help will work
- Misbelief must be finally shattered

**Point 5: Execution of New Plan**
Victory through change:
- Hero does something they couldn't do before
- Transformation is proven
- External goal achieved (or meaningfully lost)
- Internal need met

---

### 15. FINAL IMAGE (99-100%)

**Purpose:** Visual "after" snapshot showing transformation.

**Must Be:**
- Mirror opposite of Opening Image
- Clear proof of change
- Visual/action-based (showing)
- Single scene

**Questions to Ask:**
- How far has the hero come?
- What do they know now they didn't before?
- How is their life different?
- Would readers recognize this as opposite of the beginning?

## Transformation Test Checklist

For each beat, verify:

**Opening Image**
- [ ] Single scene or connected scenes
- [ ] Visual (showing, not telling)
- [ ] Flaws evident

**Theme Stated**
- [ ] Relates to hero's need/lesson
- [ ] Stated by non-hero
- [ ] Hero dismisses it

**Setup**
- [ ] At least one thing needs fixing
- [ ] A Story characters introduced
- [ ] Want/goal established
- [ ] Hero in multiple life areas
- [ ] Flaws visible
- [ ] Stasis = death pressure

**Catalyst**
- [ ] Happens TO hero
- [ ] Action beat (not revelation)
- [ ] Prevents return to status quo
- [ ] Big enough to break the world

**Debate**
- [ ] Can be summed as a question
- [ ] Shows hesitation
- [ ] Multiple life areas addressed

**Break Into 2**
- [ ] Hero leaves old world
- [ ] Act 2 is opposite of Act 1
- [ ] Clear threshold crossing
- [ ] Proactive decision
- [ ] Based on WANT (wrong way)

**B Story**
- [ ] New character introduced
- [ ] Represents theme
- [ ] Product of Act 2 world

**Fun and Games**
- [ ] Hero succeeding OR floundering
- [ ] Delivers promise of premise
- [ ] Shows upside-down world

**Midpoint**
- [ ] Clear false victory OR false defeat
- [ ] Stakes raised
- [ ] A and B stories cross
- [ ] Shift from wants to needs begins

**Bad Guys Close In**
- [ ] Path opposite of Fun and Games
- [ ] Internal bad guys (flaws) shown
- [ ] External pressure increases

**All Is Lost**
- [ ] Something happens TO hero
- [ ] Big enough for Act 3 transformation
- [ ] Whiff of death
- [ ] Like another Catalyst

**Dark Night of the Soul**
- [ ] Hero reflects
- [ ] Leads toward epiphany
- [ ] Worse off than beginning

**Break Into 3**
- [ ] Theme/lesson learned
- [ ] Proactive decision
- [ ] Based on NEED (right way)
- [ ] Act 3 synthesizes Acts 1 and 2

**Finale**
- [ ] Hero struggles
- [ ] Transformation proven through action
- [ ] Theme applied to win
- [ ] High Tower Surprise present
- [ ] Dig Deep Down moment present

**Final Image**
- [ ] Visual proof of change
- [ ] Opposite of Opening Image
